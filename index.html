A career in SEO: 27 experts share their tips & insights

Aswin Ramesh

I don’t know about the prevailing trends, but I believe being self taught is the best way to go about it. SEO as a subject is vast and is evolving everyday, therefore, I feel doing one online course alone or sticking to one handbook will not do justice to it. One needs to keep up with newer trends and changes, and for that you need to keep reading! Follow the industry leaders, subscribe to credible SEO blogs & forums, and you will do well.

@Aswin Ramesh

CEO of Synup

Shane Barker

Learning SEO can be very exciting at first. But let me give you a heads up that it is a very broad topic and there is a lot to understand about SEO.
There are many online guides, ebooks, blog posts, and PDFs about SEO available online. These materials will help you to gain some insights and knowledge about the basics of SEO. I suggest being careful when reading online info about SEO, as it may be outdated or may misguide and mislead you.
SEO trends change very quickly as Google updates their algorithm almost every day. So, I suggest that you be on your toes and always learn about the new trends in SEO.
You may want to consider online courses or degrees in SEO. Though majority of information provided in an online course can be found on the internet. But an online course can save you a lot of time and effort since you won’t have to search for the best materials yourself. Also, courses are typically well structured, which can help you stay focused.

@Shane Barker

Digital Strategist at Shane Barker

Aaron Wall

If you go back 10 or 15 years much of the industry was very open & the idea of being in the SEO industry was relatively unpopular. A lot of the independent web publishers sort of got flushed away by the Panda & Penguin updates. As wins became harder and scarcer, and the barriers to entry increased, people became stingier with sharing knowledge. There are a few folks like Glenn Gabe who still regularly share in-depth quality information & advice, though they are the exception to the rule. It is very easy to get caught up in noise reading Twitter regularly (at least as far as SEO goes).
Influencers who are sharing things you haven’t seen elsewhere are probably worth paying a bit more attention to. The people who lead discussions in a new direction & uncover things that are worth reading.
A lot of the worst spammers & most duplicitous individuals in the industry would proclaim righteously they are white hat & against spam. Those people are probably worth avoiding. Or, if you follow them, fade whatever they suggest.

@Aaron Wall

SEO Expert & founder of SEOBook

Time Soulo

There’s really no better way to acquiring competency/knowledge of SEO other than start a website of your own and try to grow its organic search traffic.

You can spend months agonising over SEO theory and try to keep in touch with the latest fads, but as soon as you try to actually apply all that knowledge you’ll realise how much of it is wheat and how much is chaff.

So if you want to get good at SEO, try to spend less time learning and more time implementing what you already know.

Then don’t forget to socialize and exchange your knowledge/experience with fellow SEOs. Most people don’t go to SEO conferences for the talks, but for the networking with fellow SEOs

@Time Soulo

Time Soulo, Head of Marketing & Product Strategy at Ahrefs

Patrick Langridge

I think it’s really important to test and try different SEO strategies to understand what does and doesn’t work. Not all strategies are effective in all niches, so the best way of developing and learning as an SEO is to get stuck in and try for yourself!

We encourage team members to dabble with their own personal projects and websites too, as this can accelerate learning a huge amount. Being self taught is certainly a great place to start, and shows future employers that you are genuinely interested in the industry and are keen to learn more, but I would still encourage working with more experienced SEOs to really get a deep understanding of the discipline.

I’m fortunate enough to have been educated in one of the most respected and trusted names in the SEO industry, being taught and trained by the best in the business. If you have same opportunity, I’d highly recommend it.

Patrick Langridge, Head of SEO at Screaming Frog

Phil Rozek

To do well in local SEO you should be largely self-taught, but you also need to get good input and experience from other sources. Here are the main ways you should learn – roughly in order of importance:

By doing local SEO for at least one paying client. (Even if you’re not good yet, and even if you don’t get paid much.)
By marketing and growing your own business. Notice I said “market,” not “do local SEO for.” Local SEO may or may not be how you get your clients, as a local SEO-er. (Ironic, huh?) The experience will keep you tethered to reality, so you don’t lose sight of what’s practical for business owners.
By paying attention to what good/longtime/ credible local SEOs say and do.
By keeping an eye on what bad/hacky SEOs do (so you can avoid it and take a different tack).
By writing down your observations, questions, findings, results, etc. Keep “lab notes.”
By finding a mentor – a longtime local SEO who’s made more mistakes than you have. It can be one person or a “brain trust” of people. (Yes, that means you probably will have to pay for their time. Less costly than the alternatives.)

@Phil Rozek

Phil Rozek, CEO of Local Visibility System

Aleksander Nedyalkov

First, there is no one-way path to SEO. Second, you need to learn:

1. Technical SEO – Navigational structures, site speed, accessibility, semantics, etc.
2. Front-end development or UX SEO – Optimizing for specific UX purposes
3. Content strategy SEO – This is bit more project management-oriented and requires a lot of coordination. It also requires a good keyword research knowledge and technical SEO understanding from the writers.
4. Link building or Outreach SEO – Knowing how to build backlinks through effective lead generation and outreach strategy is also an important skill every SEO should have.
5. Data and business analytics – Finally, none of the above SEO skills are of any value without the ability to strategize and continuously improve action plans based on careful analysis of performance metrics and business data.

I can’t say that any of these SEO learning paths is less valuable than the other. But these days, search engine algorithms are pushing towards a trend that is of natural and safe results produced by outreach SEO.

Considering all of the above, plus the fact that we’re now living in a fast-paced, constantly evolving online world, the only way to become a real expert is to really work hard, read a lot, and test continuously. This is especially effective when done alongside a team of peers whom you can always exchange knowledge and best practices with.

@Aleksander Nedyalkov

Aleksander Nedyalkov, SEO & Marketing Manager at NinjaOutreach

Adam Steele

I am not too sure, but I’d lean towards most folks being self-taught. There are some courses out there, including Brian Dean’s SEO that Works course. Courses are great, but I am sure if you asked Brian he’d tell you that maybe 5-10% of folks actually apply what he’s taught. I always start new trainees off with free information, of which there is plenty of in SEO.

Moz has a very decent beginner’s guide that will get you up to speed on the glossary of SEO terms. I will mix that with some practical knowledge i.e. go register yourself a domain, build a WordPress site on whichever topic, and optimize it to the best of your ability. Next, I’d point them in the direction of some outreach guides and templates and ask them to rank for some terms. Of course, there is a lot more to SEO, but there is a tendency for SEOs to gloss over the basics and go right to the advanced stuff. Most SEOs will be working on SMB-style, 10-20 page websites. I recommend steering clear of the advanced strategies, nail the basics and when you’re ready, build on that foundation.

@Adam Steele

Adam Steele, CEO of Loganix
